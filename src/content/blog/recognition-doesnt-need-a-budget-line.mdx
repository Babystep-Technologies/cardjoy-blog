We were reading through r/humanresources this week — a new HR manager asking for help building a recognition program from scratch. No budget yet, no plan, just the instinct that people deserve to feel appreciated.

The replies were more useful than most consulting decks we've seen.

One HR director put it plainly: don't design the program before you know how your people actually want to be recognized. Another pointed out that most employees don't want logowear or a mug — they want an extra day off, or just to be seen. Someone else, who builds recognition software for a living, said the thing that works most consistently is starting simple and focusing on frequency over size.

None of that is complicated. All of it is easy to forget once a vendor demo and a budget approval get involved.

### Small and often beats big and rare

A $500 award ceremony once a year competes with eleven months of silence. A small, specific thank-you every few weeks doesn't have to compete with anything — it's just part of how the team works. Frequency is what makes recognition feel like culture instead of an event.

### Specific beats generic

"Great job" evaporates by lunchtime. "The way you caught that pricing error before it went to the client, and stayed late to fix it without being asked" sticks around. The HR thread agreed on this almost unanimously — the dollar amount or the object matters far less than whether the message names the actual thing someone did.

### Prove it before you buy it

The failure mode nobody mentioned outright but everyone implied: buying the full platform — points, catalog, badges, integrations — before anyone's proven that people on the team will actually participate in something lightweight first. Prove the behavior. Then scale the budget toward whatever's actually getting used.

---

This is close to why we built CardJoy the way we did. A group card costs nothing to try, takes a few minutes to start, and puts everyone's actual voice into it — not one manager's canned line copy-pasted from a template. It's not trying to be the platform with the catalog and the points system. It's the lightweight version you reach for on a Tuesday, for a birthday or a farewell or just because someone had a hard week — the kind of small, frequent, specific thing that, it turns out, was the advice all along.
